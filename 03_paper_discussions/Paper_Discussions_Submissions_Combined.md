
1. Olm, et al. Figure 3b:
Figure 3B shows the percentage of microbial strains detected in mothers and infants that are found only in infants (yellow), only in mothers (green), or shared within mother–infant dyads (pink), and they are grouped by bacterial phylum.  

The figure was generated using the sequenced fecal sample data from both the infants and their mothers, which allows the researchers to identify microbes at the strain level and detect whether vertical transmission occurs. 

The main result of this figure is that some microbial strains are shared between mothers and infants, indicating some vertical transmission, however, several strains are not shared. The amount of strain sharing also differs across bacterial phyla, suggesting that vertical transmission is taxon-specific rather than uniform across the entire gut microbiome. 

In the context of the paper’s results, this figure shows that maternal transmission contributes to infant microbiome assembly but does not fully/definitively determine it. Infants must also acquire microbes from their environment. This figure is needed/helpful because it provides strain-level evidence about how the infant microbiome is assembled, helping the authors move beyond just describing microbiome differences across lifestyles to more clearly understanding the mechanisms of microbiome assembly. 

These findings may guide future researchers in refining questionnaires to identify at-risk workers and inform targeted interventions to enhance occupational health.

3. Rico 2021, Figure 4a
Figure 4a is of a Principle Component Analysis plot of the weighted UniFrac distances that visualize beta diversity, or how different the microbial communities were from one another across all three inocula, time, and the feeding treatments over the 20-day experiment with the smaller dots being closer to day 0 and the incrementally larger dots moving towards day 20. 

The dots that cluster more tightly together indicate samples with very similar microbial composition, while the dots spread further apart indicate more distinct communities. so, we can see that each inoculum forms its own little tight cluster that stays separate from the others throughout the entire experiment, even though all reactors were run under identical conditions, which tells us that the starting microbiome was really the driving force behind community structure. Within each cluster, the fed reactors gradually shift away from their Day 0 starting positions more than the unfed controls do, showing that cellulose was actively pushing the community to change, while the controls stayed relatively stable. 

The weighted UniFrac metric is worth noting too because it accounts for both which species are present and how abundant they are, and is phylogenetically informed, so, closely related species contribute less to the distance calculation than distantly related ones. & this visual matters to the grand scheme of the paper and it's findings because the fact that these clusters never overlap or converge is strong visual evidence that the initial inoculum source is a primary driver of microbiome structure, which in turn directly explains why each inoculum produced a different fatty acid profile and connects back to one of their main findings.


4. https://link.springer.com/article/10.1186/s40168-024-01886-x
Figure 2 has two parts. The first is a sunburst diagram, which has a series of rings radiating outward, where each ring represents a deeper level of taxonomic classification, starting from domain in the center and moving out to species on the outer edges. The colors correspond to the different phyla, and anywhere that you see a gap in a ring, means those organisms could not be classified at that particular level. The second part is a bar chart that counts how many MAGs hit that classification wall and at what particular level.

What both visuals are showing is that the majority of the 326 microbial genomes recovered in this study belong to groups that either currently have no formal scientific name or are only identified by a placeholder code. So, this mapping is novel because over 100 of them fall into families that have never been formally named. When the authors later show that specific microbes are producing plant growth hormones like IAA and GA4 in response to cover crop exudates, they are often talking about organisms that science has never really looked at before. That is what makes this figure 2 matter in the larger context of their results. It is not just the roll call of who is there but It is also evidence that agricultural soils contain a largely undiscovered microbial world, and that the cover crop exudates are one way to start identifying these organisms.

7. Figure 7 depicts co-occurrence correlation networks illustrating relationships between specific bacterial genera in milk and various milk components, such as the total protein for both the PC and MC groups.

The red edges indicate positive correlations, meaning that as the abundance of a specific genus increases, so does the concentration of the milk component, and the blue edges represent negative correlations, where an increase in a genus’s abundance corresponds to a decrease in the milk component.

The networks focus on specific genera that either appeared only in one group or showed significant differences in abundance between the two groups. We can see that in the PC group, several genera were positively correlated with total protein levels, suggesting that certain bacteria may enhance the milk protein content.

& the significance of this figure is that the information could be critical for improving milk production strategies based on microbial health.


8. Figure 8A
This figure shows how microbial diversity, especially bacterial diversity is related to different physical and chemical characteristics of the sourdough bread. Specifically, we are looking at pH, acidity, lactic and acetic acid, free amino acids, as well as the loaf's volume and crumb firmness. 

The y axis on the right represents the strength of the correlation so blue is strong positive correlation while red is strong negative and the lighter colors means weak or no statistically meaningful relationship. 

& What we can see is that higher bacterial diversity is linked to more fermentation products like lactic acid and amino acids, but actually a smaller loaf volume, so there’s kind of a tradeoff between flavor development and bread's structure/texture. In contrast, the fungal diversity doesn’t seem to play as big of a role here, with only a significant relationship with the PH.
