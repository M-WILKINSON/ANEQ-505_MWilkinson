1. Olm, et al. Figure 3b:
Figure 3B shows the percentage of microbial strains detected in mothers and infants that are found only in infants (yellow), only in mothers (green), or shared within mother–infant dyads (pink), and they are grouped by bacterial phylum.  

The figure was generated using the sequenced fecal sample data from both the infants and their mothers, which allows the researchers to identify microbes at the strain level and detect whether vertical transmission occurs. 

The main result of this figure is that some microbial strains are shared between mothers and infants, indicating some vertical transmission, however, several strains are not shared. The amount of strain sharing also differs across bacterial phyla, suggesting that vertical transmission is taxon-specific rather than uniform across the entire gut microbiome. 

In the context of the paper’s results, this figure shows that maternal transmission contributes to infant microbiome assembly but does not fully/definitively determine it. Infants must also acquire microbes from their environment. This figure is needed/helpful because it provides strain-level evidence about how the infant microbiome is assembled, helping the authors move beyond just describing microbiome differences across lifestyles to more clearly understanding the mechanisms of microbiome assembly. 

These findings may guide future researchers in refining questionnaires to identify at-risk workers and inform targeted interventions to enhance occupational health.

3. Rico 2021, Figure 4a
Figure 4a is a Principle Component Analysis plot of weighted UniFrac distances that visualizes the beta diversity, or how different the microbial communities were from one another across all three inocula, time points, and feeding treatments over the 20-day experiment. 

Dots that cluster tightly together indicate samples with very similar microbial composition, while the dots spread further apart indicate more distinct communities. We can see that each inoculum forms its own tight cluster that stays separate from the others throughout the entire experiment, even though all reactors were run under identical conditions, which tells us the starting microbiome was really the driving force behind community structure. Within each cluster, the fed reactors gradually shift away from their Day 0 starting positions more than the unfed controls do, showing that cellulose was actively pushing the community to change, while the controls stayed relatively stable. The weighted UniFrac metric is worth noting too because it accounts for both which species are present and how abundant they are, and is phylogenetically informed, meaning closely related species contribute less to the distance calculation than distantly related ones. This matters because the fact that these clusters never overlap or converge is strong visual evidence that the initial inoculum source is a primary driver of microbiome structure, which in turn directly explains why each inoculum produced a different fatty acid profile and connects back to one of the central findings of the paper.
